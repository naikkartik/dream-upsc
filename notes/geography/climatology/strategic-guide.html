Part 02

The Six Dimensions of Mains Questions

Every Mains question on Climatology fits into one of these six dimensions. Recognising the dimension tells you what kind of answer the examiner expects — and that's half the battle.

01 · Conceptual "What and How"

Pattern: Define a phenomenon and explain its mechanism.

Past Questions

  • 2024What is sea surface temperature rise? How does it affect the formation of tropical cyclones?
  • 2024What is the phenomenon of 'cloudbursts'? Explain.
  • 2024What are aurora australis and aurora borealis? How are these triggered?
  • 2021Troposphere is a very significant atmospheric layer that determines weather processes. How?
  • 2013What do you understand by 'temperature inversion'? How does it affect weather and habitants?

What the Examiner Wants

Crisp definition · Process flow (cause → trigger → outcome) · Diagram (mandatory) · Real-world examples · Brief consequences.

Depth required: Process-level mastery. You must know why and how, not just what.
02 · Locational "Why Here?"

Pattern: Names specific locations, asks why a phenomenon occurs there.

Past Questions

  • 2014Tropical cyclones are largely confined to South China Sea, Bay of Bengal, and Gulf of Mexico. Why?
  • 2013Major hot deserts in the northern hemisphere are located between 20–30°N latitudes and on the western side of the continents. Why?
  • 2024What is a twister? Why is the majority of twisters observed in areas around the Gulf of Mexico?

What the Examiner Wants

Identify governing factors (latitude, ocean currents, pressure systems, Coriolis, continentality) · Causal chain, not lists · Map sketch with arrows · Comparative reasoning — why here and not elsewhere.

Depth required: Hold a mental world-map and connect 4-5 factors simultaneously. This is integrative geography.
03 · Climate–Human Climate-Society Interaction

Pattern: UPSC links climatic phenomena to society, economy, culture, agriculture.

Past Questions

  • 2023Why is the SW Monsoon called 'Purvaiya' in Bhojpur Region? How has it influenced the cultural ethos?
  • 2017What characteristics can be assigned to monsoon climate that feeds >50% of world population in Monsoon Asia?
  • 2015How far do you agree that the behaviour of the Indian monsoon has been changing due to humanising landscape?
  • UndatedCauses for the formation of heat islands in urban habitats.

What the Examiner Wants

Two-part answer: half climate science + half human/social · Cultural/economic/agricultural illustrations · Specific regional examples · Forward-looking implications, adaptation.

Depth required: Cross-domain. Read newspaper case studies. UPSC rewards rooted, India-specific texture here.
04 · Climate Change The New Favourite

Pattern: UPSC's go-to dimension for the last 5 years. Almost every year has at least one question here.

Past Questions

  • 2024SST rise & tropical cyclone formation (process-meets-climate-change hybrid).
  • 2023Consequences of climate change on food security in tropical countries.
  • 2021Melting of Arctic ice vs glaciers of Antarctic — differential weather and human-activity effects.
  • 2020Desertification does not have climatic boundaries. Justify.
  • 2019Impact of global warming on coral life system with examples.
  • 2017How does the cryosphere affect global climate?
  • UndatedShrinking Himalayan glaciers and symptoms of climate change in the subcontinent.

What the Examiner Wants

Current data (IPCC AR6, latest WMO reports) · Both global mechanism + Indian context · Specific examples (Joshimath, Chamoli, Sundarbans, Lakshadweep) · Adaptation/mitigation angle · Linkage to govt schemes/COP outcomes.

Depth required: Highest. Without current data, your answer is generic (4–5/10). With data, you score 8–10/10.
05 · Variability ENSO, IOD, Air Masses, WDs

Pattern: UPSC tests teleconnections — how distant ocean-atmosphere phenomena drive Indian weather.

Past Questions

  • 2014Most unusual climatic happenings are explained as an outcome of the El-Niño effect. Do you agree?
  • 2016Discuss the concept of air mass and explain its role in macro-climatic changes.

What the Examiner Wants

Conceptual clarity (normal state vs disturbed state) · Walker Circulation diagram for ENSO · Counter-balancing arguments for "do you agree" questions · Other co-factors: IOD, MJO, Arctic Oscillation · Linkage to specific Indian events.

Depth required: High. Master teleconnections — how Pacific SST anomalies affect Indian monsoon. Memorise: +IOD = good monsoon, El Niño = poor monsoon (with nuances).
06 · Current Affairs Named-Event Questions

Pattern: UPSC takes a recent climate event and tests both the event AND the underlying concept.

Past Questions

  • 2022Color-coded weather warnings for cyclone-prone areas by IMD.
  • UndatedThe recent cyclone called 'Phailin'. How are tropical cyclones named globally?

What the Examiner Wants

Specifics of the event (date, place, intensity) · Institutional knowledge (IMD 4-stage warning, WMO naming, RSMC New Delhi) · Map/diagram of affected region · Policy implications.

Depth required: Medium but recency-critical. Read IMD press releases and last 12 months of cyclone/extreme weather news.

01
What is the phenomenon of 'temperature inversion' in meteorology? How does it affect the weather and habitats of the place?
Mains 2013 · 5 marks · 100 words · Conceptual
+

Intro (1-2 lines)

Normally, atmospheric temperature decreases with altitude (Normal Lapse Rate: 6.5°C/km). Temperature inversion is a reversal of this gradient — temperature rises with height in a specific layer.

Body — Types

  1. Radiational inversion — clear winter nights, rapid surface heat loss
  2. Air-drainage (valley) inversion — cold air sinks into valleys; Kashmir, Shimla
  3. Advectional inversion — warm air moves over cold surface
  4. Frontal inversion — warm air rises over cold air at fronts
  5. Subsidence inversion — anticyclonic descending air

Body — Effects

  • Weather: Fog formation, smog (Delhi winter), suppressed convection, no rainfall
  • Habitat: Frost damages crops, respiratory diseases (PM2.5 trapped), aviation hazards. But valley inversion benefits horticulture (apples in Kashmir thrive on chilling).

Conclusion

Temperature inversion is a sharp reminder that vertical atmospheric structure governs surface weather as decisively as horizontal pressure systems.

02
Tropical cyclones are largely confined to the South China Sea, Bay of Bengal, and Gulf of Mexico. Why?
Mains 2014 · 250 words · Locational
+

Intro

Tropical cyclones require a strict combination of thermal, dynamic, and humidity conditions, which are met only in specific oceanic zones.

Common Factors in These Three Regions

  1. SST ≥ 26.5°C up to 60-70 m depth — provides latent heat reservoir
  2. Latitudinal sweet spot (5°-20°) — Coriolis adequate but not too high; near equator Coriolis is too weak
  3. Pre-existing low-pressure disturbance (ITCZ proximity) — provides seed convection
  4. Low vertical wind shear — allows vertical organisation of the storm
  5. High mid-tropospheric humidity — prevents dry-air intrusion
  6. Funnelling coastlines — BoB and Gulf of Mexico both have a concave funnelling shape that intensifies storm surge

Why These Three Specifically?

  • Bay of Bengal: shallow continental shelf, warm semi-enclosed basin, monsoon trough as trigger
  • South China Sea: warm Kuroshio extension, persistent low-pressure, peak frequency Aug-Oct
  • Gulf of Mexico: warm Loop Current, easterly waves from Africa, hurricane alley

Why NOT Others?

  • South Atlantic — high wind shear, no ITCZ, cold Benguela Current → almost no cyclones
  • South-east Pacific — Humboldt cold current suppresses convection

Conclusion

The geography of warm oceans, Coriolis-favourable latitudes, and trigger systems collectively concentrates tropical cyclogenesis into these three basins, while making others almost cyclone-free.

03
How far do you agree that the behaviour of the Indian monsoon has been changing due to humanising landscapes? Discuss.
Mains 2015 · 12 marks · 200 words · Climate-Human
+

Intro

"Humanising landscapes" refers to large-scale anthropogenic transformation of natural surfaces — urbanisation, deforestation, irrigation, industrialisation. Mounting evidence shows these are altering the Indian monsoon's spatial and temporal behaviour.

Agreement — How the Monsoon IS Changing

  1. Aerosols & condensation nuclei: Industrial pollution increases nuclei → urban areas receive disproportionately heavy rainfall (Mumbai 2005, Chennai 2015), rural areas dry
  2. Land-use change: Deforestation in Western Ghats and NE India reduces evapotranspiration → weakened orographic rainfall
  3. Urban heat islands: Delhi, Mumbai create localised low-pressure → shifted rainfall cells
  4. Black carbon over IGP: weakens Tibetan-plateau heating → delayed monsoon onset
  5. Sea-surface warming: Anthropogenic CO₂ → warmer Arabian Sea → more cyclones (Tauktae, Biparjoy) disrupting monsoon

Caveats — Not Only Humans

  • ENSO, IOD, MJO, solar variability play decisive roles
  • Decadal natural variability cannot be ignored

Conclusion

While natural drivers remain dominant, humanising landscapes have unambiguously shifted the distribution, intensity, and predictability of monsoon — making adaptation policy as crucial as climate diplomacy.

04
Discuss the concept of air mass and explain its role in macro-climatic changes.
Mains 2016 · 12 marks · 200 words · Variability
+

Intro

An air mass is a large body of air (1000+ km horizontally, several km vertically) with relatively uniform temperature and humidity characteristics, acquired from its source region.

Classification

TypeSourceCharacter
cA / cAAArctic / AntarcticCold, dry
cPContinental polar (Siberia, Canada)Cold, dry, stable
mPMaritime polar (N Atlantic, N Pacific)Cold, moist
cTContinental tropical (Sahara, Australia)Hot, dry
mTMaritime tropical (Gulf of Mexico, Indian Ocean)Hot, moist
mEMaritime equatorialWarm, very moist

Role in Macro-Climatic Changes

  1. Frontogenesis: Where contrasting air masses meet → fronts → temperate cyclones → European/N-American winter storms
  2. Heat waves: cT air masses displaced poleward → European 2003, India 2015, 2022, 2024 heat waves
  3. Cold waves: cP air masses pushed southward → cold waves in N India ("Polar vortex collapse")
  4. Western Disturbances: mP air masses originating over Mediterranean bring N-Indian winter rainfall
  5. Monsoon mechanism: mT air mass invading Indian subcontinent IS the SW monsoon
  6. Rossby wave meandering: enables air masses to penetrate unusual latitudes — driver of extreme events under climate change

Conclusion

Air masses are the dynamic units of climate; their movement, modification, and collisions shape both daily weather and decadal climatic shifts.

05
Most of the unusual climatic happenings are explained as an outcome of the El Niño effect. Do you agree?
Mains 2014 · 10 marks · 150 words · Critical Thinking
+

Intro

El Niño — a quasi-periodic warming of the equatorial Pacific — undoubtedly influences global weather, but attributing most unusual climatic events to it overstates its role.

Yes, El Niño Explains a Lot

  • Indian monsoon droughts (2002, 2009, 2014-16)
  • Australia bushfires
  • Peru anchovy fishery collapse, South American flooding
  • Sub-Saharan droughts
  • Reduced Atlantic hurricanes
  • Coral bleaching (1998, 2016)

But Other Drivers Also Matter

  • Indian Ocean Dipole: positive IOD rescued Indian monsoon in El Niño years (2019)
  • Madden-Julian Oscillation: intra-seasonal rainfall variability
  • Arctic Oscillation / Polar Vortex: European winters
  • Atlantic Multi-decadal Oscillation: decadal Sahel rainfall
  • Anthropogenic climate change: independent rising trend in extremes
  • Volcanic eruptions: Tambora "year without summer"

Conclusion

El Niño is a dominant but not exclusive driver. Modern climatology recognises a multi-system coupling — over-attribution to El Niño obscures the true complexity. The most rigorous answer: El Niño is necessary but not sufficient.

06
How do the melting of the Arctic ice and glaciers of the Antarctic differently affect the weather patterns and human activities on the Earth?
Mains 2021 · 15 marks · 250 words · Climate Change
+

Intro

The cryosphere — frozen water systems — is the fastest-changing component of the climate system, but Arctic and Antarctic are reacting very differently with very different downstream consequences.

Arctic Melting (Sea ice + Greenland)

Weather Pattern Effects:

  1. Polar amplification — Arctic warming 4x global average
  2. Weakened jet stream → meandering Rossby waves → extreme winters in N America/Europe, heatwaves in Russia
  3. Polar vortex disruption — Beast from the East (2018), Texas freeze (2021)
  4. AMOC slowdown — meltwater from Greenland disrupts thermohaline circulation

Human Activities:

  • Opening of Arctic shipping routes (NSR, NW Passage)
  • Resource scramble (Russia-NATO-China geopolitics)
  • Indigenous livelihood loss (Inuit, Sami)
  • Methane emission from permafrost — accelerator feedback

Antarctic Melting (Land ice)

Weather Pattern Effects:

  1. Global sea-level rise (Antarctic holds 90% of Earth's ice)
  2. Southern Ocean freshening — disrupts Antarctic bottom water formation
  3. Wind shifts — Southern Annular Mode positive trend
  4. Less impact on jet streams (Southern Ocean is more zonally uniform)

Human Activities:

  • Coastal inundation threat to S Asia, Pacific Islands, Sundarbans
  • No indigenous population displacement (uninhabited)
  • Antarctic Treaty system pressures (no resource exploitation yet)
  • Krill fishery decline → cascading ecosystem impact

Contrast

Arctic = atmospheric weather disruption + geopolitical opening; Antarctic = sea-level catastrophe.

Conclusion

Bipolar asymmetry means humanity faces a twin crisis: weather chaos from the north, drowning coastlines from the south. Mitigation must address both, but adaptation must prepare for them separately.

07
What is sea surface temperature rise? How does it affect the formation of tropical cyclones?
Mains 2024 · 10 marks · 150 words · Hybrid
+

Intro — Definition

Sea Surface Temperature (SST) is the water temperature near the ocean's surface (top 1m). Globally, SST has risen by ~0.88°C since 1900 (IPCC AR6), with the rise accelerating since 1980. The Indian Ocean is warming faster than any other ocean basin (~1°C in the last century).

How SST Rise Affects Cyclone Formation

  1. More energy availability: Cyclones extract latent heat from warm oceans; +1°C SST raises potential intensity by ~5%
  2. Expanding cyclogenesis areas: Earlier cyclone-free Arabian Sea now produces severe cyclones (Tauktae 2021, Biparjoy 2023, Mocha 2023)
  3. Faster intensification: Rapid Intensification (RI) events doubled since 1980; cyclones now jump categories in <24 hours (Fani, Amphan)
  4. Wetter cyclones: Warmer atmosphere holds more moisture (Clausius-Clapeyron: 7% per °C) — extreme rainfall (Hudhud, Phailin)
  5. Poleward shift of tracks: Cyclones reaching higher latitudes (Cyclone Idai-Mozambique, Mediterranean medicanes)
  6. Longer cyclone season: Pre-monsoon Arabian Sea cyclones now extend into May (earlier was rare)
  7. Higher storm surges: Combined effect of stronger cyclones + sea-level rise

Conclusion

SST rise is the single most decisive climate-change link to tropical cyclones, transforming the Indian Ocean from a "secondary" basin into a high-risk cyclone zone — demanding upgraded IMD forecasting and coastal disaster planning.

08
Troposphere is a very significant atmospheric layer that determines weather processes. How?
Mains 2021 · 15 marks · 250 words · Conceptual
+

Intro

The troposphere is the lowermost atmospheric layer, extending from the surface to ~8 km at poles and ~18 km at equator. It contains 75% of atmospheric mass and 99% of water vapour — making it the sole arena of weather.

Why Troposphere "Determines" Weather

  1. Water vapour residence — All cloud-rain processes (condensation, precipitation) occur here
  2. Normal Lapse Rate (6.5°C/km) — declining temperature drives convection
  3. Vertical mixing & convection — Buoyancy-driven uplift creates thunderstorms, cyclones
  4. Latent heat exchange — Condensation releases 540 cal/g, powering all storms
  5. Pressure systems — Highs, lows, anticyclones, depressions all form in the troposphere
  6. Wind systems — Trade winds, monsoons, westerlies operate here
  7. Tropopause as ceiling — limits vertical extent of weather; cyclones cannot punch above
  8. Jet streams at tropopause — STJ and PFJ steer surface weather and synoptic systems
  9. Greenhouse effect localised here — Water vapour and CO₂ trap longwave radiation → climate regulation
  10. All air masses & fronts — global air mass interaction is tropospheric

Why the Other Layers Don't Drive Weather

  • Stratosphere — stable, dry, inversion layer; only ozone chemistry
  • Mesosphere — too thin, only meteor burning
  • Thermosphere — auroras, but no moisture

Conclusion

The troposphere is not just a layer where weather happens — it is the only layer where weather can happen. Its thermal and dynamic properties make it the engine room of climate.

09
Bring out the relationship between the shrinking Himalayan glaciers and the symptoms of climate change in the Indian sub-continent.
Mains undated · 10 marks · 150 words · Climate Change
+

Intro

Himalayan glaciers — the "Third Pole" — store the largest cryospheric mass outside polar regions. Their shrinkage (estimated 0.3-0.4 m water equivalent per year) is both a symptom and a driver of subcontinental climate change.

Glaciers as Symptom

  1. Accelerated retreat: Gangotri (~22 m/yr), Pindari, Siachen
  2. Snowline upward shift — ~30-50 m higher in 30 years
  3. Glacier debris cover increasing — Hindu-Kush-Himalaya assessment

Glaciers as Driver of Symptoms

  1. GLOFs: Kedarnath 2013, Chamoli 2021, Sikkim 2023 — direct climate-change-driven disasters
  2. Altered river regimes — Brahmaputra, Indus, Ganga lean-season flows declining
  3. Western Disturbance intensification — Warmer mid-troposphere strengthens winter precipitation extremes
  4. Indian Monsoon shifts — Tibetan Plateau snow cover anomalies modulate monsoon strength
  5. Permafrost thaw — methane release, slope instability (Joshimath)
  6. Agricultural stress — kharif failure in Indo-Gangetic plain
  7. Biodiversity shifts — snow leopards, blue sheep ranges contracting

Conclusion

Himalayan cryosphere is the canary in the coal mine — its decline foreshadows water insecurity, disaster intensification, and ecological collapse across the subcontinent.

10
What characteristics can be assigned to monsoon climate that succeeds in feeding more than 50 percent of the world population residing in Monsoon Asia?
Mains 2017 · 15 marks · 250 words · Climate-Society
+

Intro

Monsoon Asia — including India, China, SE Asia, Korea, Japan — hosts ~55% of global population and 80% of global rice production. The monsoon climate's specific characteristics make this extraordinary food density possible.

Characteristics Enabling Food Security

  1. Seasonal concentration of rainfall (75-90% in 4 months) — Aligns with crop kharif cycle; single intense wet season → flood-irrigation possible for paddy
  2. High total annual rainfall (1000-3000 mm) — Crop water needs met without exhaustive irrigation; surplus available for downstream agriculture
  3. High temperatures during growing season (25-35°C) — Photosynthesis maximised; multiple cropping (2-3 cycles annually)
  4. High humidity — Reduces crop water stress; favours paddy, sugarcane, jute, tea
  5. Predictable seasonality — Pre-modern societies could plan sowing/harvest; festivals and cultural calendar aligned (Onam, Pongal, Mid-Autumn)
  6. River systems fed by monsoon + glaciers — Year-round water for Ganga, Indus, Mekong, Yangtze basins; alluvial soils renewed annually
  7. Mild winters in plains — Allows rabi (wheat, mustard) cropping; effectively doubles agricultural output

Challenges

  • Inter-annual variability (drought-flood swings)
  • Climate change intensifying extremes

Conclusion

The monsoon's combination of heat, water, predictability, and seasonality created the demographic miracle of Monsoon Asia — but rising climate variability threatens this 5000-year-old food-security architecture.

11
What is the phenomenon of 'cloudbursts'? Explain.
Mains 2024 · 10 marks · 150 words · Conceptual
+

Intro — Definition

A cloudburst is an extreme rainfall event with intensity ≥100 mm per hour over a small area (<20-30 km²), often producing localised flash floods. They are common in mountainous tropics, especially the Himalayas.

Mechanism

  1. Orographic uplift — Warm, moist air forced rapidly up steep slopes
  2. Conditional instability — Air becomes saturated; Cumulonimbus tower develops
  3. Trapping of cloud — Mountain topography prevents the cloud from drifting
  4. Coalescence + collision — Strong updrafts hold raindrops aloft; they grow large
  5. Sudden collapse — When the updraft weakens, all accumulated water falls in minutes
  6. Cumulonimbus heights of 12-15 km — explosive release

Conducive Factors (especially Himalayan)

  • Steep slopes (Uttarakhand, HP, J&K)
  • Monsoon trough interaction
  • Warming-induced atmospheric moisture (Clausius-Clapeyron)
  • Western Disturbance interaction in late monsoon

Impacts

  • Flash floods (Leh 2010, Kedarnath 2013, Amarnath 2022, Wayanad 2024)
  • Landslides, GLOFs triggered
  • High casualty due to suddenness

Conclusion

Cloudbursts are localised but catastrophic — and climate change is increasing both their frequency and unpredictability, demanding micro-scale forecasting and early-warning capacity in Himalayan states.

12
Bring out the causes for the formation of heat islands in the urban habitat of the world.
Mains undated · 5 marks · 100 words · Climate-Human
+

Intro

Urban Heat Island (UHI) refers to urban areas being 2-7°C warmer than surrounding rural areas, particularly at night.

Causes

  1. Albedo reduction: Concrete, asphalt (5-15% albedo) replace vegetation (20-25%)
  2. Heat retention: Building materials absorb and re-emit heat slowly
  3. Anthropogenic heat: Vehicles, AC compressors, industries release waste heat
  4. Reduced evapotranspiration: Loss of vegetation → less latent heat absorption
  5. Reduced sky-view factor: Tall buildings trap longwave radiation (urban canyon effect)
  6. Air pollution: Aerosols absorb radiation
  7. Reduced wind: Buildings break airflow → poor heat dispersal
  8. Impervious surfaces: Rainwater runs off rapidly, no cooling

Conclusion

UHIs are growing globally as urbanisation expands; mitigation requires cool roofs, urban forestry, blue-green infrastructure, and porous surfaces.

13
The process of desertification does not have climatic boundaries. Justify with examples.
Mains 2020 · 10 marks · 150 words · Climate Change
+

Intro

Desertification — land degradation in drylands due to climate and human factors — was historically considered a Saharan/Thar phenomenon. UNCCD and recent science show its spread well beyond classical arid zones.

Justification

  1. Cold deserts undergoing further desertification: Ladakh, Gobi, Patagonia
  2. Semi-arid → arid drift: Marathwada, Bundelkhand, Sahel
  3. Sub-humid → semi-arid: Karnataka, Andhra interiors, parts of MP
  4. Humid tropics: Amazon, Congo deforestation = "savannisation"
  5. Coastal salinisation: Sundarbans (Bangladesh-India), Vietnamese Mekong
  6. Mountain desertification: Spiti Valley, Hindu Kush
  7. Cold-arctic tundra degradation: Permafrost thaw destroys land productivity
  8. Mediterranean climate desertification: Spain, Portugal, parts of California

Drivers Crossing Climatic Boundaries

  • Overgrazing (Mongolia, Rajasthan)
  • Deforestation (Brazil, Indonesia)
  • Soil salinity (irrigation excess — Punjab, Uzbekistan)
  • Climate change (rainfall shifts everywhere)

Conclusion

Desertification is now a global phenomenon — proof that human pressure has made land degradation climate-agnostic, demanding the integrated approach UNCCD's LDN (Land Degradation Neutrality) framework envisions.

14
What are aurora australis and aurora borealis? How are these triggered?
Mains 2024 · 15 marks · 250 words · Conceptual
+

Intro — Definition

Auroras are spectacular natural light displays in polar skies — aurora borealis in the Arctic (Northern Lights) and aurora australis in the Antarctic (Southern Lights). They occur predominantly in the thermosphere (100-400 km altitude).

Triggering Mechanism (Step by Step)

  1. Origin in the Sun:
    • Solar wind — continuous stream of charged particles (electrons, protons) ejected from sun's corona at ~400 km/s
    • Solar flares and Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) intensify the wind
  2. Interaction with Earth's magnetosphere:
    • Solar wind compresses the magnetosphere
    • Charged particles travel along Earth's magnetic field lines toward polar regions
  3. Entry through polar cusps:
    • Particles funnel into upper atmosphere near magnetic poles
    • Geographic ovals at 60-75° latitude
  4. Excitation of atmospheric gases:
    • Charged particles collide with oxygen and nitrogen atoms
    • Atoms get "excited" — electrons jump to higher orbits
  5. Photon emission (the visible light):
    • Atoms return to ground state, releasing photons
    • Oxygen at 100-300 km → green/yellow (most common)
    • Oxygen above 300 km → red
    • Nitrogen → blue/purple

Why Only Polar Regions?

  • Earth's magnetic field lines plunge into the atmosphere at the poles
  • Charged particles can only reach atmosphere at these "magnetic cusps"

Triggering Events of Strong Auroras

  • Geomagnetic storms (CME-driven) — Carrington Event 1859, May 2024 storm visible up to Ladakh
  • Solar maximum (11-year cycle) — current cycle peak ~2024-25
  • HF radio disruptions, satellite damage, power grid risks

Conclusion

Auroras are visible reminders of the Sun-Earth electromagnetic relationship — beautiful but indicators of space weather hazards relevant to satellites, aviation, and power grids in our increasingly tech-dependent civilisation.

Diagram: Sun → solar wind → magnetosphere → polar cusps → atmospheric excitation.
15
Discuss the meaning of colour-coded weather warnings for cyclone-prone areas given by the Indian Meteorological Department.
Mains 2022 · 10 marks · 150 words · Current Affairs
+

Intro

The Indian Meteorological Department (IMD) uses a four-tier colour-coded warning system to communicate cyclone and severe-weather risks, enabling graded administrative and public response. Adopted globally (WMO impact-based forecasting).

The Four Tiers

ColourMeaningPublic ActionAdmin Action
GREENNo warning, normal weatherNoneRoutine monitoring
YELLOW"Be aware" — bad weather possibleMonitor forecastsDistrict alert, equipment readiness
ORANGE"Be prepared" — high impact (heavy rain, gusts >65 kmph)Reduce travel, stock essentialsNDRF/SDRF mobilised, school closures considered
RED"Take action" — extreme impact (winds >115 kmph, cyclone landfall)Evacuate, stay indoorsFull evacuation, shelters opened, transport halted

Forecast Components

  • Lead time: 5-day forecast, 24-hour nowcast
  • Wind, rainfall, storm surge issued separately
  • District-level granularity since 2019

Importance

  • Phailin (2013) and Fani (2019) showed minimal deaths — IMD warnings + evacuation saved 1M+ lives
  • Critical for fishermen advisory, ports, aviation, agriculture

Challenges

  • Last-mile communication in remote coasts
  • Smaller cyclones with rapid intensification (Tauktae 2021)
  • Compound events (cyclone + heavy rainfall + storm surge)

Conclusion

Colour-coded warnings represent India's evolution from "deterministic forecast" to "impact-based, decision-ready" early-warning — a global model recognised by the WMO.
