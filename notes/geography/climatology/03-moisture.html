Moisture & Precipitation

How water gets into the atmosphere, becomes clouds, falls as rain — and the extreme forms it takes: thunderstorms, cloudbursts, and the recently-discovered atmospheric rivers that drown coastlines.

Humidity & Evaporation
PDF · Ch 24
+

Definition

Humidity = water vapour content in air. Evaporation = liquid water → vapour. Driving force of the hydrological cycle.

Types of Humidity

  • Absolute humidity — actual mass of water vapour per unit volume (g/m³)
  • Specific humidity — mass of water vapour per unit mass of air
  • Relative humidity (RH) — percentage of max moisture air can hold at that temperature
  • Dew point — temperature at which RH = 100%

Factors Affecting Evaporation

  1. Temperature — higher temp = more evaporation
  2. Humidity — drier air = more evaporation
  3. Wind speed — removes saturated layer → more evaporation
  4. Water-surface area — more surface = more evaporation
  5. Composition of water — saline slower than fresh

Clausius–Clapeyron Relationship

Air's water-holding capacity increases ~7% per °C of warming. This is the fundamental reason climate change leads to more intense precipitation events.

Wet-Bulb Temperature

Combined measure of heat and humidity — when wet-bulb crosses 35°C, human survival is impossible even in shade. South Asia is approaching this threshold during peak summers.

Condensation & Cloud Formation
PDF · Ch 24
+

Definition

Phase change of water vapour to liquid water (or directly to ice = deposition). Requires (1) air cooled to dew point, and (2) condensation nuclei (dust, salts, aerosols, pollution particles).

Cooling Mechanisms

  1. Adiabatic cooling — rising air (orographic, convective, frontal, cyclonic) — main mechanism
  2. Radiational cooling — heat loss at night → fog, dew
  3. Advection — warm air over cold surface → fog
  4. Mixing of air masses — at fronts

Forms of Condensation

FormDescriptionConditions
DewWater droplets on surfacesClear night, calm air, high humidity
FrostIce crystals on surfacesDew point below freezing
FogCloud at ground level (visibility < 1 km)Cooling + condensation nuclei
MistLess dense (visibility > 1 km)Similar, lighter
CloudsCondensation aloftAdiabatic uplift

Cloud Types (4 Families)

  • High clouds (6–12 km) — Cirrus, Cirrostratus, Cirrocumulus (ice crystals)
  • Mid clouds (2–6 km) — Altostratus, Altocumulus
  • Low clouds (0–2 km) — Stratus, Stratocumulus, Nimbostratus
  • Vertical development — Cumulus, Cumulonimbus (thunderstorms)

Climate Change Linkage

Aerosol pollution increases condensation nuclei → can suppress rain (small droplets don't coalesce) or cause intense bursts. Urban pollution alters local cloud microphysics. Cloud feedback remains the single largest uncertainty in climate models.

Types of Rainfall
PDF · Ch 24
+

Definition

Rainfall = precipitation in liquid form. Classified by the mechanism causing air uplift.

Three Types

TypeMechanismExample Regions
ConvectionalSurface heating → warm air rises → cools → condensesEquatorial belt, Amazon, Congo
OrographicAir forced up windward slopes of mountainsWestern Ghats, Himalayas, Cherrapunji
Cyclonic / FrontalWarm air rises over cold air at frontsMid-latitudes, Britain, NW Europe

Orographic Rainfall — Key Concepts

  • Windward side: heavy rainfall (Cherrapunji ~11,800 mm/yr)
  • Leeward side: rain shadow (Pune behind Western Ghats; Ladakh behind Greater Himalayas)
  • Foehn winds: warm dry winds descending leeward (Chinook in Rockies, Foehn in Alps)

Climate Change Linkage

Rainfall is becoming more intense, less frequent. India's monsoon — total rainfall stable, but heavy-rain days increasing 3×, moderate days decreasing. Greater flood + drought risk simultaneously.

Thunderstorms
PDF · Ch 25
+

Definition

A short, violent storm with heavy rain, lightning, thunder, sometimes hail, produced by a cumulonimbus cloud. Lifespan: 30 minutes to a few hours.

Conditions Required

  1. Plenty of moisture (high humidity)
  2. Conditional instability (warm humid surface, cool aloft)
  3. Trigger mechanism (surface heating, frontal lifting, orographic)

Life Cycle (3 Stages)

  1. Cumulus stage: updrafts dominate, cloud grows
  2. Mature stage: updrafts + downdrafts; rain, lightning, hail
  3. Dissipating stage: downdrafts dominate, rain weakens, cloud collapses

Types

  • Thermal (single-cell) — afternoon storms; brief
  • Frontal — at cold/warm fronts
  • Orographic — at mountain barriers
  • Mesoscale Convective System (MCS) — large multi-cell, long-lasting
  • Supercell — rotating updraft (mesocyclone); spawns tornadoes

Indian Examples

  • Kalbaisakhi / Nor'westers — pre-monsoon thunderstorms, West Bengal/Odisha
  • Mango showers — pre-monsoon, Karnataka/Kerala
  • Loo + dust storms — N India pre-monsoon (Andhi)
  • Western Disturbance thunderstorms — N India winter

Climate Change Linkage

Higher SST + more moisture → more intense thunderstorms. Lightning strikes increasing in India — 2,500+ deaths/year, concentrated in Odisha, Bihar, Jharkhand, MP. NDMA notified lightning as a disaster in 2023.

Cloudbursts
PDF + PMF IAS Detailed Mains 2024
+

Definition

An extreme localised rainfall event with intensity ≥100 mm/hour over an area <20–30 km², producing flash floods. Almost always associated with deep cumulonimbus over mountainous terrain.

Mechanism (Step-by-Step)

  1. Orographic uplift: warm moist air forced rapidly up steep slopes
  2. Conditional instability: parcel reaches dew point, condensation begins
  3. Cumulonimbus tower grows to 12–15 km vertically
  4. Strong updrafts hold raindrops suspended; droplets coalesce, grow large
  5. Topographic trapping: surrounding mountains prevent cloud from drifting
  6. Updraft collapse: when moisture supply weakens, water mass dumps in minutes

Conducive Factors

  • Steep slopes (Himalayas, Western Ghats, NE hills)
  • Monsoon trough or Western Disturbance interaction
  • High atmospheric moisture (warm SST upstream)
  • Clausius–Clapeyron: each +1°C → +7% moisture available

Notable Indian Cloudbursts

  • Leh 2010 — 257 mm in 2 hours; 250+ deaths
  • Kedarnath 2013 — 375 mm in 24 hrs + GLOF; 5,000+ deaths
  • Amarnath Yatra 2022 — 31 mm in <1 hr; 17 deaths
  • Wayanad 2024 — cloudburst-triggered landslides; 400+ deaths

Climate Change Linkage

Warmer atmosphere = more moisture; warmer Arabian Sea/Bay of Bengal = more moisture transport; weakened jet streams = stalled monsoon troughs over Himalayas. Cloudbursts are becoming more frequent and unpredictable.

Mitigation

  • Doppler weather radar expansion (IMD's Mission Mausam)
  • Nowcasting (3–6 hour forecasts) for Himalayan states
  • Restricted construction in floodplains
  • Early-warning sirens in pilgrim/tourist zones
  • Slope stabilisation, afforestation

Atmospheric Rivers (Pineapple Express)
PMF IAS New
+

Definition

Long, narrow corridors of concentrated water vapour in the atmosphere (~400–500 km wide, 1000+ km long) that transport moisture from tropical oceans to higher latitudes. Often called "rivers in the sky."

Mechanism

  1. Warm tropical oceans evaporate vast amounts of water
  2. Cyclonic winds organise vapour into a narrow band
  3. The river extends thousands of km, flowing poleward
  4. On hitting coastal mountains, vapour is forced up → torrential rain
  5. Single event can carry water equal to 7–15× the daily flow of the Mississippi

Famous Atmospheric Rivers

  • Pineapple Express — tropical Pacific (near Hawaii) → US West Coast
  • Atmospheric Pineapple — Atlantic equivalent affecting UK/Europe
  • Indian Monsoon ARs — increasingly identified during Kerala 2018, Chennai 2015 floods

Where They Occur

  • North Pacific (US West Coast)
  • North Atlantic (Western Europe)
  • Southeast Pacific (Chile)
  • South Atlantic
  • Indian subcontinent (emerging area of study)

Dual Nature — Beneficial vs Destructive

BeneficialDestructive
40–50% of US West Coast water supply80% of flooding damage on West Coast
Replenishes drought-stricken regionsTriggers landslides, mudflows
Snowpack accumulation (Sierra Nevada)Coastal erosion
Sustains hydroelectric reservoirsLevee breaches, urban flooding

Climate Change Linkage

Warmer oceans + more atmospheric moisture (Clausius–Clapeyron) → atmospheric rivers becoming longer, wider, wetter. By 2100, AR intensity may increase 25%. India's recent extreme rainfall events (Kerala 2018, Pakistan floods 2022) involved AR-like moisture transport.
