Pressure, Wind & Circulation

How pressure differences drive air movement, how Earth's rotation deflects it, how planetary circulation organises winds into trade, westerly, and polar regimes — with jet streams overhead and Rossby waves bending them out of shape.

07
Atmospheric Pressure & Wind
PDF · Ch 23 Prelims 1997, 2011
+

Definition

Atmospheric pressure = weight of air column above a unit area. Sea level average = 1013 hPa (mb). Falls ~1 hPa per 8m altitude initially.

Factors Affecting Pressure

  1. Temperature — heating expands air → low pressure; cooling → high pressure
  2. Altitude — pressure falls with altitude
  3. Humidity — moist air lighter than dry → lower pressure
  4. Earth's rotation — creates pressure belts

Seven Global Pressure Belts

BeltLatitudeTypeCause
Equatorial Low (Doldrums)0–5°Thermal lowStrong heating
Subtropical High (Horse Latitudes)25–35°N/SDynamic highSubsiding Hadley cell air
Sub-polar Low60–65°N/SDynamic lowCoriolis-driven divergence
Polar High80–90°N/SThermal highExtreme cold

Types of Winds

  • Permanent (planetary): Trade winds, Westerlies, Polar Easterlies
  • Secondary (periodic): Monsoons, Land–sea breeze, Mountain–valley breeze
  • Local: Loo (N India), Foehn (Alps), Chinook (Rockies), Mistral (Rhone), Sirocco (Sahara → S Europe), Bora (Adriatic), Harmattan (W Africa)

Climate Change Linkage

Hadley cell expansion is shifting subtropical highs poleward (~50-100 km/decade) → expanding deserts. Pressure belts no longer stationary in their classical positions.

Coriolis Force & Geostrophic Wind
PDF · Ch 23 Prelims 2010
+

Definition

Coriolis force is an apparent (pseudo) force caused by Earth's rotation that deflects moving objects to the right in NH and left in SH. Zero at equator, maximum at poles.

Why It Exists

Earth rotates at 1670 km/h at equator but slower at poles. A moving parcel retains its eastward speed; moving north (in NH), the ground below moves slower → parcel appears to curve right.

Geostrophic Wind

  • Wind flowing parallel to isobars in upper atmosphere (above friction layer)
  • Result of Coriolis balancing pressure gradient force
  • Jet streams are essentially geostrophic winds

Key Consequences

  1. Cyclones rotate anti-clockwise in NH, clockwise in SH
  2. Anticyclones rotate the opposite way
  3. Tropical cyclones cannot form within 5° of equator (Coriolis too weak)
  4. Trade winds deflect to NE and SE (not direct N–S)
  5. Ocean gyres rotate opposite ways in two hemispheres
  6. SH Westerlies stronger (Roaring 40s, Furious 50s) due to less landmass friction

General Circulation — Three-Cell Model
PDF · Ch 23 Mains 2013
+

Definition

Global atmospheric circulation organised into three vertically-stacked cells per hemisphere: Hadley (tropical), Ferrel (mid-latitude), Polar — together moving heat from equator to poles.

The Three Cells

CellLatitudeMechanismSurface Winds
Hadley0–30°N/SRising at ITCZ → poleward aloft → subsiding at 30° (subtropical high)Trade winds (NE/SE)
Ferrel30–60°N/SSurface poleward, rising at ~60°, returning aloft (driven by neighbouring cells)Westerlies
Polar60–90°N/SSubsiding cold air at pole → equatorward → rising at 60°Polar Easterlies

ITCZ — Intertropical Convergence Zone

Where NH and SH trade winds meet. Marked by intense convection, thunderstorms, doldrums. Shifts seasonally — northward in N-summer (drives Indian SW monsoon), southward in N-winter.

Climate Change Linkage

Expansion of Hadley cell (~50–100 km/decade) — pushes subtropical highs poleward → expanding deserts (Sahara, US Southwest, Australia drying). Weakening Ferrel cell → erratic mid-latitude weather.

Jet Streams
PDF · Ch 27 Prelims 2011
+

Definition

Narrow, fast-flowing rivers of air in the upper troposphere (~9–12 km altitude), moving west-to-east at 100–400 km/h. Form where strong temperature gradients exist between air masses.

Types

Jet StreamLocationRole
Sub-Tropical Jet (STJ)~25–35°N/SSteers Western Disturbances; controls Indian monsoon onset/withdrawal
Polar Front Jet (PFJ)~50–60°N/SSteers temperate cyclones; meandering causes extremes
Tropical Easterly Jet (TEJ)~10–15°N, summerDrives Indian SW monsoon; flows east-to-west (Africa → India)
African Easterly Jet~15°N, summerSets up easterly waves → Atlantic hurricanes
Somali Jet (low-level)Surface, monsoonCarries moisture from Arabian Sea to India

Indian Monsoon Mechanics

  • Pre-monsoon: STJ over N India suppresses monsoon
  • Onset: STJ withdraws north across Himalayas → TEJ establishes → monsoon bursts
  • Withdrawal: STJ returns south → TEJ weakens → monsoon retreats

Climate Change Linkage

Arctic warming reduces equator-pole gradient → weakened, meandering jet stream (Rossby waves amplify) → polar vortex disruption → extreme winters (Texas 2021) and heatwaves (Europe 2022). Western Disturbance intensification linked to slower STJ.

11
Rossby Waves
PDF · Ch 27
+

Definition

Large-scale meanders in the polar front jet stream caused by Coriolis effect and temperature contrasts. They undulate around the globe in 3–6 large waves, separating warm tropical air from cold polar air.

Mechanism

  1. Jet stream encounters temperature gradient at polar front
  2. Coriolis force induces oscillation — wave pattern develops
  3. Each "ridge" carries warm tropical air poleward
  4. Each "trough" carries cold polar air equatorward
  5. Waves can amplify or break — leading to extreme weather

Impact on Weather

  • Steers temperate cyclones along the jet's path
  • Cold air outbreaks — when troughs dip deeply south
  • Heat waves — when ridges extend deeply north
  • Atmospheric blocking — when waves stall (next topic)
  • Twin cyclones — Rossby + MJO interaction

Climate Change Linkage

Arctic amplification narrows equator-pole gradient → jet stream weakens and meanders more → larger-amplitude Rossby waves → more frequent extremes.

Atmospheric Blocking
Web Research New
+

Definition

A persistent, slow-moving high-pressure system that "blocks" the normal west-to-east flow of weather systems for days to weeks. Acts like a boulder in a river, forcing weather to flow around it.

Types of Blocking Patterns

TypeStructureEffects
Omega Block (Ω)High flanked by two lows — Greek letter shapeHeat domes; persistent dry weather
Rex BlockHigh over a low (dipole)Stuck weather; cold or hot
Cut-off LowDetached low-pressure systemPersistent rain; cold snap

Mechanism — How Blocks Form

  1. Rossby wave amplifies into a deep ridge
  2. The ridge becomes detached from the main westerly flow
  3. Forms a closed anticyclonic circulation
  4. Persists 7–30 days, sometimes longer
  5. Subsiding air → clear skies → solar heating amplifies → heatwave

Notable Events

  • European heatwave 2003 — Omega block, 70,000 deaths
  • Russian heatwave 2010 — extended block, wildfires
  • Pacific Northwest heat dome 2021 — Omega block, 49.6°C in Canada
  • Europe heatwave 2022 — UK touched 40°C for first time
  • India heatwave May 2024 — partial blocking pattern over N India

Climate Change Linkage

Weakened jet stream + Arctic amplification → blocks become more frequent and persistent. Linked to "Quasi-Resonant Amplification" theory where waves get trapped in mid-latitudes.
