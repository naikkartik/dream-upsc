Variability — Ocean–Atmosphere Oscillations

The named teleconnections driving global weather on seasonal-to-interannual timescales — ENSO, IOD, MJO, and the increasingly important marine heatwaves. Master these to understand why some monsoons fail and others flood.

26
El Niño & Walker Circulation
PDF · Ch 29 Mains 2014
+

Definition

El Niño = quasi-periodic warming of central and eastern equatorial Pacific Ocean SSTs (every 2–7 years). The oceanic component of ENSO (El Niño–Southern Oscillation).

Walker Circulation — Normal State

  1. Trade winds push warm surface water westward → piles up over Indonesia/Australia ("Warm Pool")
  2. Cold deep water upwells off Peru (cold tongue)
  3. Air rises over Indonesia (low pressure) → heavy rainfall
  4. Air sinks over Eastern Pacific (high pressure) → dry deserts (Atacama)
  5. Closed loop = Walker Cell

El Niño State

  1. Trade winds weaken or reverse
  2. Warm Pool slumps eastward toward Peru
  3. Walker Circulation weakens/reverses
  4. Rising air shifts to central/east Pacific (heavy rain Peru/Ecuador)
  5. Sinking air over Indonesia/India/Australia (drought)

Global Impacts

  • Indian monsoon weakening — droughts 2002, 2009, 2014–16, 2023
  • Peru floods, Australia bushfires, Indonesia drought
  • Reduced Atlantic hurricanes (more shear)
  • Coral bleaching globally (1998, 2016, 2023)
  • Mild winter in N USA

Variants & Indicators

  • El Niño Modoki — warming concentrated in central Pacific. Different teleconnections.
  • SOI = Tahiti pressure − Darwin pressure; negative SOI = El Niño
  • ONI = 3-month avg SST anomaly in Niño 3.4 region (≥ +0.5°C for 5 months = El Niño)

La Niña
PDF · Ch 29 Prelims 2011
+

Definition

The opposite of El Niño — abnormally cool SSTs in central/eastern equatorial Pacific. Trade winds stronger than normal; Walker Cell intensified.

Mechanism

  1. Trade winds strengthen → push warm water further west
  2. Deeper, stronger upwelling off Peru
  3. Western Pacific Warm Pool expands
  4. Walker Cell amplified
  5. Cold tongue extends further into central Pacific

Global Impacts

  • Strong Indian monsoon — surplus rainfall (2020, 2021, 2022, 2024)
  • Australia flooding (2010–11, 2022 Brisbane)
  • Indonesia heavy rains
  • More Atlantic hurricanes (less shear)
  • Drought in southern USA, SE Africa
  • Cold winters in N USA
  • Peru anchovy fishery boom

Triple-Dip La Niña (2020–22)

An unusual three consecutive La Niña years — only the third on record. Associated with successive Australia floods, East Africa drought (worst in 40 years), and surplus Indian monsoons. Possible climate-change influence under debate.

Climate Change Linkage

Some studies suggest more frequent extreme La Niñas; others suggest a "more El Niño-like" mean state. The 2020–22 triple-dip raised concerns about prolonged La Niña regimes under warming.

Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD)
PDF · Ch 29 Prelims 2017
+

Definition

Difference in SST between Western Indian Ocean (near Africa) and Eastern Indian Ocean (near Indonesia). Discovered 1999. The Indian Ocean's own ENSO-like phenomenon.

Phases

PhaseSST PatternIndian MonsoonAfrican Impact
Positive IODWarmer West, Cooler EastEnhanced monsoonFloods in E Africa
Negative IODCooler West, Warmer EastWeakened monsoonDroughts in E Africa
NeutralNormal gradientNormal (subject to ENSO)Normal

How It Saves the Indian Monsoon

  • Positive IOD can offset El Niño — 2019 was a strong El Niño year but India had near-normal monsoon due to super positive IOD
  • Negative IOD with El Niño = worst-case drought (2002)
  • Positive IOD with La Niña = bumper monsoon

Mechanism

  1. Easterly winds along equator weaken
  2. Warm pool spreads to Western Indian Ocean
  3. Cold upwelling near Sumatra/Indonesia
  4. Convection shifts to Western IO → more rain reaching India

2019 & 2024 IOD Events

2019: Strongest positive IOD in 60 years; rescued Indian monsoon during El Niño; devastated Australia (bushfires) and East Africa (locust swarms).
2024: Neutral-to-positive IOD aligned with strong 2024 monsoon (108% of normal).

Madden–Julian Oscillation (MJO)
PDF · Ch 29 Detailed
+

Definition

An eastward-moving belt of enhanced rainfall (convection) and suppressed rainfall that traverses the equatorial Indian and Pacific Oceans every 30–60 days. The largest element of intra-seasonal tropical variability.

8 Phases

MJO has 8 phases, each 5–10 days, indicating position of the active convective region:

  • Phases 2–3 (over Indian Ocean) — boost Indian monsoon rainfall
  • Phases 4–5 (Maritime Continent — Indonesia/Malaysia) — break in Indian monsoon
  • Phases 6–7 (Western Pacific) — weaken Indian monsoon further
  • Phases 8–1 (Africa, Western Hemisphere) — recovery

Differences from ENSO

  • MJO is moving, ENSO is stationary
  • MJO timescale: weeks; ENSO: months–years
  • Multiple MJO events per year; one ENSO every 2–7 years
  • MJO modulates ENSO (can trigger El Niño initiation through westerly wind bursts)

Impacts

  • Modulates Indian monsoon — active/break phases
  • Tropical cyclone formation — convective phase favours genesis
  • Twin cyclones — MJO + Rossby waves
  • Cold air outbreaks — alters jet stream globally
  • Heatwaves — induces atmospheric Rossby waves
  • El Niño initiation — westerly wind bursts

Climate Change Linkage

Studies suggest MJO is intensifying and slowing under climate change → more extended wet/dry periods. Linked to extreme rainfall events globally and increased Arabian Sea cyclogenesis.

Marine Heatwaves (MHWs)
PMF IAS · Web New
+

Definition

Periods of abnormally high ocean surface temperatures lasting ≥5 consecutive days, with SST above the 90th percentile of the local 30-year climatology. Can last weeks to years.

Mechanism — How They Form

  1. Weak winds reduce normal ocean cooling (evaporation + mixing)
  2. Stratification — top layer becomes thin, isolated from cooler depths
  3. Solar heating concentrates in thin layer → rapid warming
  4. Atmospheric blocking often overhead — high pressure suppresses cooling
  5. Can be amplified by El Niño, IOD, or local feedbacks

Categories (Hobday et al. 2018)

  • Category I — Moderate: 1–2× temperature anomaly
  • Category II — Strong: 2–3×
  • Category III — Severe: 3–4×
  • Category IV — Extreme: >4× (e.g., "Blob" in NE Pacific 2013–16)

Notable Events

  • "The Blob" — NE Pacific 2013–16; killed millions of seabirds, salmon
  • 2023 N Atlantic MHW — record-breaking, 5°C above normal
  • 2024 Arabian Sea MHW — fuelled severe Indian heatwave; coral bleaching in Gulf of Mannar, Lakshadweep
  • 2023–24 global event — 4th global coral bleaching event (NOAA)

Impacts

  • Coral bleaching — Lakshadweep, Andamans, Gulf of Mannar
  • Fish kills — direct thermal stress on commercial species
  • Species migration — tropicalisation of temperate seas
  • Fuel for cyclones — rapid intensification (Tauktae 2021, Mocha 2023)
  • Atmospheric heat waves — MHW + heat dome compound events
  • Disrupted monsoon — Arabian Sea MHWs alter moisture transport

Climate Change Linkage

MHWs have doubled in frequency since 1980. At +2°C global warming, MHWs become 23× more likely. Indian Ocean MHWs have tripled.
