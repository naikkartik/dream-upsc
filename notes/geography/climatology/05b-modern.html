Heat Domes
PMF IAS · Web New
+

Definition

A high-pressure atmospheric system that traps hot air over a region for an extended period, acting like a "lid" or "dome" — causing record-breaking heatwaves. Closely linked to Omega blocking patterns.

Mechanism

  1. High-pressure system stalls due to atmospheric blocking (Omega block)
  2. Air subsides from upper troposphere → compresses adiabatically → warms
  3. Clear skies → solar radiation reaches surface unimpeded
  4. Surface heats → upward radiation absorbed by overhead high-pressure
  5. Land becomes dry → reduced evapotranspiration → more heat retained
  6. Positive feedback: hotter ground → drier ground → more heating

Notable Heat Domes

  • Pacific Northwest June 2021 — 49.6°C in Lytton, BC; hundreds dead; town burnt down next day
  • Russia 2010 — Moscow 38.2°C; 55,000 deaths; massive wildfires
  • Europe 2003 — France/Italy/Spain heatwave; 70,000 deaths
  • India May 2024 — Delhi 52.9°C reading; record heat across Rajasthan, NW India
  • Texas/Mexico June 2023 — exceptional heat dome

Why Climate Change Worsens Them

  • Warmer baseline temperatures
  • Weakened jet stream → more blocking
  • Soil moisture deficits amplify heat
  • Compound with droughts → worse outcomes

Impacts

  • Mass mortality (especially elderly, urban poor)
  • Wildfire ignition (Canada 2023 record-breaking fires)
  • Power grid strain (AC demand surge)
  • Crop failures, livestock deaths
  • Glacier melt acceleration
  • Marine heatwave amplification

Polar Vortex & Stratospheric Sudden Warming
PMF IAS · Web New
+

Definition

Polar Vortex = large-scale region of low pressure and cold air swirling around both poles. Two types: stratospheric polar vortex (15–50 km altitude) and tropospheric polar vortex (lower). Strongest in winter.

Stratospheric Sudden Warming (SSW)

A dramatic event where the stratospheric polar vortex weakens or splits, accompanied by sudden temperature rise (sometimes +50°C in a few days) in the polar stratosphere.

Mechanism:

  1. Rossby waves from troposphere propagate upward into stratosphere
  2. Waves break and deposit westerly momentum
  3. Westerly polar vortex winds slow, reverse to easterly
  4. Vortex weakens, splits, or displaces
  5. Cold polar air spills into mid-latitudes 1–3 weeks later

Consequences (Northern Hemisphere)

  • Severe cold air outbreaks 1–3 weeks after SSW event
  • Texas freeze February 2021 — followed January SSW; deadly
  • "Beast from the East" 2018 — UK/Europe paralysed
  • Indian winter cold waves — when polar air dips far south
  • Disrupts jet stream → weather extremes
  • Increases ozone in polar stratosphere temporarily

"Warm Arctic, Cold Continents" Paradigm

Counter-intuitive climate-change paradigm: Arctic amplification + sea-ice loss may increase frequency of SSW events — paradoxically causing more severe winters in mid-latitudes even as the planet warms.

●Conceptual"What is the polar vortex? Explain stratospheric sudden warming (SSW) and its triggers."
●Locational"Why does an SSW in the Arctic deliver winter chaos to Texas, Europe, and even North India?"
●Climate-Human"Discuss the cascading impact of SSW events on power grids, transportation, and public health in mid-latitudes."
●Climate Change"Examine how Arctic amplification under climate change is destabilising the polar vortex more often."
●Variability"The 'warm Arctic, cold continents' paradigm — explain how a warming planet can deliver more severe winters."
●Current Affairs"The Texas freeze of February 2021 — examine the role of polar vortex disruption and lessons for grid resilience."

AMOC Slowdown & The "Cold Blob"
Web Research New
+

Definition

AMOC = Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation. A massive ocean conveyor that carries warm surface water from tropics to N Atlantic, where it cools, sinks, and returns south at depth. Powers ~25% of N Hemisphere heat transport.

Mechanism

  1. Gulf Stream carries warm water northeast across Atlantic
  2. In N Atlantic, water cools, becomes dense (and saltier from evaporation)
  3. Dense water sinks near Greenland and Labrador Sea
  4. Travels southward at depth as North Atlantic Deep Water
  5. Eventually upwells in Southern Ocean and Indian/Pacific Oceans
  6. Cycle takes ~1000 years to complete

Why It's Slowing

  • Greenland ice sheet melting — freshens N Atlantic surface
  • Fresher water = less dense = doesn't sink → conveyor weakens
  • Arctic sea-ice loss adds more freshwater
  • Increased rainfall in high latitudes (climate change)
  • AMOC has weakened ~15% since 1950 (multiple studies)

The Cold Blob

A persistent cold patch of ocean south of Greenland — the only major region cooling while the rest of the planet warms. Evidence of AMOC weakening.

Consequences

  • European heatwaves — cold blob shifts pressure patterns, channels warm air into Europe
  • Sea-level rise on US East Coast — slower current pushes water shoreward
  • Sahel rainfall changes — altered ITCZ position
  • Tropical Atlantic warming — more hurricanes
  • Arctic ice loss accelerates
  • If AMOC collapses — Europe could cool 5–15°C; massive global disruption

Tipping Point Debate

  • Some 2023 studies suggest tipping by 2050–2100
  • 2025 Nature study suggests AMOC unlikely to fully collapse this century
  • Even partial slowdown disruptive
  • IPCC AR6: "very likely" AMOC will weaken over 21st century

6 Dimensions of Possible Mains Questions

●Conceptual"What is the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation? Explain how thermohaline density drives it."
●Locational"Why is the only major cooling region on a warming planet located in the North Atlantic ('Cold Blob')?"
●Climate-Human"Discuss the catastrophic consequences for European agriculture, fisheries, and energy security if AMOC collapses."
●Climate Change"Examine the scientific debate on AMOC tipping points: is collapse this century plausible?"
●Variability"How does AMOC variability influence Atlantic hurricane activity and Sahel rainfall?"
●Current Affairs"Examine recent 2023–25 research debating whether AMOC slowdown has crossed a critical threshold."

If the ocean's biggest conveyor belt stalls, the planet's heat distribution machine breaks down — and we don't know exactly when that line will be crossed.

Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs)
PMF IAS New
+

Definition

A sudden release of water from a glacial lake, typically caused by failure of the natural dam (moraine or ice) holding the lake. Releases millions of cubic metres of water + debris downstream.

How Glacial Lakes Form

  1. Glacier retreats due to warming
  2. Meltwater accumulates in the depression left behind
  3. Held back by moraine (rock-debris dam) or ice barrier
  4. Lake grows as more ice melts
  5. Dam weakens with time

Triggers

  • Earthquakes (Sikkim, Nepal regions seismically active)
  • Ice/rock avalanches into the lake (impulse waves)
  • Heavy rainfall / cloudburst
  • Lake overfilling due to rapid meltwater inflow
  • Dam degradation (piping erosion, slumping)
  • Glacier surges

Notable Indian GLOFs

  • Kedarnath, June 2013 — Chorabari Lake breach + cloudburst; 5,000+ deaths
  • Chamoli, February 2021 — rock-ice avalanche → Rishiganga GLOF; 200+ deaths; NTPC hydropower destroyed
  • Sikkim, October 2023 — South Lhonak Lake breach; Teesta River flood; Chungthang hydro-dam breached; 100+ deaths

India's Vulnerability

  • HKH region lost >40% glacial mass in 40 years (ICIMOD)
  • Sikkim alone has 300+ glacial lakes, 10 highly dangerous
  • 27,000+ glacial lakes mapped across Indian Himalayas
  • NDMA's National GLOF Risk Mitigation Programme — 195 lakes prioritised, 4 risk levels

Mitigation Strategies

  • Hazard mapping — high-risk lakes prioritised
  • Early Warning Systems — automated weather + water stations
  • Lake-level lowering — controlled drainage (siphon tubes)
  • Downstream engineering — check dams, slope stabilisation
  • Regional cooperation — with China, Nepal, Bhutan
  • Restrictions on hydropower infrastructure in GLOF zones (post-Chamoli debate)

●Conceptual"What are Glacial Lake Outburst Floods? Explain how glacial lakes form and the triggers for their breach."
●Locational"Why are the Himalayan, Andean, and Patagonian regions the world's GLOF hotspots?"
●Climate-Human"Discuss the threat GLOFs pose to Himalayan hydropower projects, pilgrim routes, and downstream populations."
●Climate Change"Examine how accelerating glacier retreat is multiplying GLOF risk across the HKH region."
●Variability"How does the interaction of cloudbursts, earthquakes, and rock-ice avalanches create cascading GLOF disasters?"
●Current Affairs"The 2023 South Lhonak (Sikkim) GLOF and 2021 Chamoli disaster — examine and discuss NDMA's National GLOF Risk Mitigation Programme."

Heat Waves & Urban Heat Islands
PDF + PMF IAS Mains 2013 (UHI)
+

Heat Wave — IMD Definition

  • Maximum temperature ≥ 40°C (plains), 37°C (coast), 30°C (hills)
  • OR departure from normal ≥ 5°C (heat wave) / ≥ 7°C (severe heat wave)
  • OR actual max temp ≥ 45°C (regardless of departure)

Causes of Heat Waves

  1. Subsiding air (anticyclone/heat dome)
  2. Hot continental tropical air masses from Iran-Pakistan
  3. Loo winds over N India
  4. Soil moisture deficit — amplifies surface heating
  5. Atmospheric blocking (Omega blocks)
  6. El Niño years tend to be hotter for India

India's Heat Hotspots

Rajasthan, Vidarbha, Telangana, Odisha, Bihar, eastern UP. 2024 was India's hottest year on record — heatwave lasted unprecedented 30–50 days in some regions.

Urban Heat Island (UHI)

Urban areas 2–7°C warmer than surrounding rural areas, especially at night. Mains 2013 question on this.

Causes:

  • Albedo reduction (concrete, asphalt 5–15% vs vegetation 20–25%)
  • Heat retention by building materials
  • Anthropogenic heat (vehicles, AC, industries)
  • Reduced evapotranspiration (impervious surfaces)
  • Reduced sky-view factor (urban canyons trap radiation)
  • Air pollution absorbs heat
  • Reduced wind dispersal

Impacts

  • Heat stroke, dehydration, mortality (esp. elderly, outdoor labourers)
  • Crop wilting, livestock deaths
  • Power grid stress (AC demand spike)
  • Water shortages, reservoir evaporation
  • Increased ground-level ozone pollution

Mitigation

  • National Heat Action Plans (Ahmedabad 2013 model — first in India)
  • Cool roofs (high-albedo paint)
  • Urban forestry, green corridors
  • Blue–green infrastructure
  • Early warning (IMD colour-coded alerts)
  • Modified work hours for labourers
  • Push to notify heat wave as a national disaster (NDMA debate)

●Climate Change"Examine how compound heat–humidity extremes are pushing parts of India toward unliveable wet-bulb thresholds."
