Storms & Cyclones

The most destructive expressions of atmospheric energy — tropical cyclones, tornadoes, temperate cyclones, and the Western Disturbances that bring rain to north India in winter.

19
Tropical Cyclones
PDF · Ch 26 Mains 2014, 2022, 2024
+

Definition

Intense low-pressure systems with organised thunderstorm activity, originating over warm tropical oceans, with sustained winds >62 km/h (cyclonic storm) to >220 km/h (super cyclone). Called hurricanes (Atlantic), typhoons (Pacific), cyclones (Indian Ocean), willy-willy (Australia).

Six Conditions for Formation

  1. SST ≥ 26.5°C up to 60–70 m depth
  2. Coriolis force adequate (5°–20° latitude; absent near equator)
  3. Pre-existing low-pressure disturbance (ITCZ proximity)
  4. Low vertical wind shear (allows vertical organisation)
  5. High mid-tropospheric humidity (no dry-air intrusion)
  6. Upper-level divergence (lets convection rise)

Structure

  • Eye — calm centre, 20–50 km wide, clear skies, lowest pressure
  • Eyewall — strongest winds, deepest convection (10–20 km thick)
  • Rain bands — spiral outward, several hundred km

Three Cyclone Basins (Mains 2014)

  • Bay of Bengal — shallow shelf, semi-enclosed, monsoon trough trigger
  • South China Sea — warm Kuroshio extension, Aug–Oct peak
  • Gulf of Mexico — warm Loop Current, African easterly waves

Why not South Atlantic? High wind shear, cold Benguela current, no ITCZ.

Naming Convention

RSMC New Delhi names cyclones in North Indian Ocean (13 countries — Bangladesh, India, Maldives, Myanmar, Oman, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Thailand, UAE, Yemen + Iran, Qatar, Saudi Arabia added 2018).

Recent Indian Cyclones

Phailin (2013), Hudhud (2014), Ockhi (2017), Fani (2019), Amphan (2020), Tauktae, Yaas (2021), Biparjoy, Mocha (2023), Remal, Dana (2024).

Climate Change Linkage

SST rise → more energy → faster intensification (RI doubled since 1980); poleward shift; longer season; Arabian Sea cyclones tripled; wetter cyclones (Clausius–Clapeyron); higher storm surges with sea-level rise.

Tornadoes & Twisters
PDF · Ch 25 Mains 2024
+

Definition

A violently rotating column of air extending from a cumulonimbus cloud to the ground. Winds 65–500 km/h. Diameter 10–3,000 m. Lifespan minutes to an hour.

Tornado vs Tropical Cyclone

FeatureTornadoTropical Cyclone
SizeSmall (m to km)Huge (hundreds of km)
LifespanMinutesDays
OriginLand thunderstormOcean
Wind speedUp to 500 km/hUp to 300 km/h
Damage radiusNarrow pathWide area

Formation (Supercell Tornadoes)

  1. Warm moist surface, cool dry aloft — instability
  2. Wind shear creates horizontal vortex
  3. Strong updraft tilts the horizontal vortex vertically
  4. Mesocyclone (rotating thunderstorm) forms
  5. Funnel cloud descends; touches ground = tornado

Tornado Alley & Gulf Coast Twisters (Mains 2024)

  • Constant moisture from Gulf SST
  • Cold continental polar air mass from north
  • Strong upper-level jet stream
  • Flat plains — no mountain disruption
  • Spring + early summer peak

Indian Tornadoes

Rare. Occasional in West Bengal, Odisha, Bihar (Nor'wester regions). Tornado in WB 2024 killed 5; rare but increasing reports.

Air Masses
PDF · Ch 28 Mains 2016
+

Definition

A vast body of air (1000+ km horizontally, several km vertically) with uniform temperature and humidity characteristics, acquired by staying long enough over a source region.

Classification

SymbolTypeSource Region
cA / cAAContinental Arctic/AntarcticGreenland, Antarctica — cold, dry
cPContinental PolarSiberia, N Canada — cold, dry, stable
mPMaritime PolarN Atlantic, N Pacific — cold, moist
cTContinental TropicalSahara, Australia — hot, dry
mTMaritime TropicalGulf of Mexico, Indian Ocean — hot, moist
mEMaritime EquatorialTropical seas — warm, very moist

Role in Weather

  • Heat waves — cT pushed poleward (India 2024)
  • Cold waves — cP pushed equatorward (Texas 2021)
  • Frontogenesis — when contrasting air masses meet
  • Indian SW Monsoon = mT air mass from Indian Ocean
  • Western Disturbances = mP from Mediterranean
  • Hurricanes = mT over warm Atlantic

Fronts & Frontogenesis
PDF · Ch 28
+

Definition

A front is the boundary surface where two air masses of different properties meet. Frontogenesis = formation; Frontolysis = dissipation.

Four Types

TypeMechanismWeather
Cold FrontCold air pushes under warm air (steep slope)Brief intense thunderstorms; clears quickly
Warm FrontWarm air rides over cold air (gentle slope)Prolonged moderate rain; gradual clearing
Stationary FrontNeither air mass movesPersistent cloudy weather; rain spells
Occluded FrontCold front overtakes warm front (lifts it off ground)End stage of temperate cyclone

Frontogenesis Conditions

  1. Two contrasting air masses converging
  2. Strong temperature/humidity gradient (baroclinic zone)
  3. Jet stream above (provides upper-level divergence)

Where Fronts Form

  • Polar Front (~60°N/S) — temperate cyclones form here
  • Arctic Front — between Arctic and Polar air masses
  • Mediterranean Front — feeds Western Disturbances
  • ITCZ — equatorial "front" of trade wind convergence

Temperate Cyclones (Extratropical)
PDF · Ch 28
+

Definition

Large low-pressure systems (~1000–4000 km) of mid-latitudes (35°–65°), formed at the polar front. Driven by baroclinic instability (horizontal temperature gradient), not by warm ocean.

Life Cycle (Polar Front Theory)

  1. Stationary front — polar front quiet
  2. Wave stage — small perturbation in jet stream creates a kink
  3. Mature stage — defined warm and cold fronts develop
  4. Occlusion stage — cold front overtakes warm front
  5. Dissipation — pressure rises, cyclone fades

Tropical vs Temperate Cyclones

FeatureTropicalTemperate
OriginWarm oceanPolar front
EnergyLatent heat (condensation)Temperature gradient (baroclinic)
Size200–2000 km1000–4000 km
MovementSlow, irregularSteady, west-to-east
Lifespan7–14 days5–10 days
FrontsNo frontsWarm + cold fronts
EyePresentAbsent

Where They Affect

  • Europe — Britain, France, Scandinavia
  • USA/Canada — Great Lakes storms, blizzards
  • Mediterranean — Western Disturbances (special category)
  • Russia, Siberia
  • SH — Roaring 40s storms

Western Disturbances
PDF + PMF IAS Detailed
+

Definition

Extra-tropical storms originating over the Mediterranean Sea, moving eastward and reaching North-West India in winter (Dec–April), bringing rain and snow. Steered by the Sub-Tropical Westerly Jet (STJ).

Mechanism

  1. Cold polar air meets warm Mediterranean air → Mediterranean cyclones
  2. STJ at ~25–35°N steers them eastward
  3. Cross Iran, Afghanistan, Pakistan → enter NW India
  4. Forced uplift against Himalayas → precipitation
  5. Average 4–6 WDs per winter

Effects on India

  • Winter rainfall — Punjab, Haryana, Delhi, W UP — vital for rabi crop (wheat, mustard)
  • Snowfall in J&K, HP, Uttarakhand — replenishes glaciers
  • Cold waves in N India (post-WD)
  • Frontal thunderstorms March–April (pre-monsoon)
  • Fog formation over IGP
  • Cloud bursts when WD interacts with monsoon trough
  • Avalanches in Himalayas (Siachen)

Changing Patterns (Climate Change)

  • WD frequency increased ~50% in last 4 decades
  • Shifting northward (less rain to Punjab plains, more snow to Himalayas)
  • Becoming more intense → extreme precipitation events
  • Reaching deeper into summer months (April–June) — unusual
  • Linked to Joshimath subsidence (2023), Sikkim GLOF (2023), Kashmir avalanche (2024)

Wider Significance

  • Glacier sustenance — Himalayan glaciers depend on WD snowfall
  • Indus River system — WD-fed snowmelt powers Pakistan agriculture
  • Air quality — WDs can clear Delhi smog (with rain)
  • Tourism — Kashmir winter tourism depends on snowfall

Twin Cyclones
PDF · Ch 26
+

Definition

Two tropical cyclones forming simultaneously on opposite sides of the equator — one in each hemisphere — usually mirror images of each other.

Mechanism (MJO + Rossby Wave)

  1. Madden–Julian Oscillation produces eastward-moving convective burst near equator
  2. Equatorial Rossby waves are excited symmetrically about the equator
  3. Convection organises into twin lows on either side of the equator
  4. Westerly wind bursts between the twins sometimes also trigger El Niño
  5. Each low intensifies into a tropical cyclone — rotating in opposite directions due to Coriolis

Notable Events

  • Cyclone Asani (NH) + Cyclone Karim (SH) — May 2022, both in Indian Ocean
  • Tauktae + 22S — May 2021
  • Frequency: roughly 1–2 events per year in Indian Ocean

Climate Change Linkage

Warmer Indian Ocean → more favourable conditions for twin formation. MJO intensification under climate change increases twin probability.
