0Orientation & Why This Topic Matters

An ordinance is temporary law made by the executive (President or Governor) when the legislature is not in session. It is constitutionally legitimate — but it sits on a fault line where the executive starts doing the legislature’s job. That tension is exactly what examiners probe.

The topic is so valued because it is a single hook that lets you discuss almost every core constitutional doctrine at once: Separation of Powers, Checks & Balances, Judicial Review, Parliamentary Democracy, Federalism and Constitutional Morality. Master it once and you have ready material for many different questions.

Previous Year Questions to anchor your prep

2015 Framed around ordinance-making power vs the Separation of Powers. The question did not simply ask “should the power be abolished?” — it probed whether using the power fundamentally challenges the constitutional division of functions between executive and legislature. Even a majority government issuing several ordinances in a year raises this (e.g. the Citizenship Act ordinance). Investigative angles: rationale, role of the Supreme Court, essential conditions, legality of re-issuance, abolition vs retention.

2022 Shifted focus to the Governor’s legislative power and the legality/practice of re-issuing ordinances — framed by the landmark 2017 Supreme Court judgment (Krishna Kumar Singh).

For 2025–26, expect short questions that still demand deep, comprehensive understanding rather than superficial knowledge.

1The Three Core Gaps (the spine of the topic)

Every problem with ordinances reduces to three basic questions. Hold these in your head — the entire case law and analysis hangs off them.

GapThe questionThe risk if it fails
1. Urgency? → Misuse?Is there genuine, immediate urgency — or is the power being used to bypass Parliament?If no real urgency, the ordinance bypasses the legislature; the courts must then judge the executive’s intent.
2. Re-issue? → Limit?Are there limits on how often / under what conditions an ordinance can be re-promulgated?Constant re-issuance lets the executive govern by ordinance continuously, undermining the legislature.
3. Lapse / End? → Effect?Once an ordinance lapses or ends, do the changes it made survive or also lapse?Legal uncertainty — raising questions of Parliamentary Democracy and Arbitrariness.
The conflict that follows

Questioning the executive’s intent immediately triggers conflicts with Separation of Powers (SoP), Checks & Balances (CBs) and Judicial Review (JR). Because the judiciary is the body that checks an ordinance’s validity, it becomes the arbiter of whether the executive’s intent was genuine urgency or a misuse of power.

Issues can be split into Theoretical / Constitutional (how ordinances interact with SoP, Parliamentary Democracy, Rule of Law) and Practical (the real-world application and actual consequences of how they are used).

2Foundations: Rule of Law & Constitutionalism

The whole critique of ordinances is ultimately a test of whether the Rule of Law is being upheld. The chain runs:

Rule of Law  →  predominance of legal spirit  →  Constitutionalism  →  basis of the Constitution

Rule of Law implies
  • AoA — Absence of Arbitrary power
  • EBL — Equality Before Law
  • PLS — Predominance of Legal Spirit

A failure in any of these signals a deficiency in the Rule of Law — this is the “RoL test” you apply to ordinances.

Basic features engaged

Written Constitution · Separation of Powers · Checks & Balances · Judicial Review · Independent Judiciary · Parliamentary Democracy · Federalism.

Ordinances are themselves subject to the Rule of Law — even executive actions must operate within the legal framework (e.g. in criminal law).

3WHAT — Definition & Constitutional Provisions

This layer is your introduction and also overlaps directly with the Prelims syllabus — master the technicalities early.

Article 123 — President

Ordinance-making power during the recess of Parliament, rooted in the legislative powers of the executive head.

Article 213 — Governor

Ordinance-making power during the recess of the State Legislature.

trap Many textbooks get these numbers wrong — memorise 123 (President) and 213 (Governor) precisely.

Article 123 in detail

(1) Conditions for promulgation:

  • Can be exercised at any time except when both Houses of Parliament are in session.
  • The President must be satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary to take immediate action.
  • The President may promulgate such ordinances as the circumstances appear to require.

(2) Legal status & parliamentary oversight:

  • An ordinance has the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament.
  • Mandatory: every ordinance must be laid before both Houses.
  • It ceases to operate at the expiration of six weeks from the reassembly of Parliament — or earlier, if both Houses pass resolutions disapproving it (on the passing of the second resolution).
Temporal limit — the number to remember

Maximum duration of an ordinance = 6 months + 6 weeks. The logic: once the legislature reassembles, the ordinance must be passed within 6 weeks; if not, it lapses. This strict limit is precisely why excessive re-promulgation became the central abuse.

Other key provisions & limits

  • Governor’s subject-matter limit: the Governor cannot issue an ordinance on matters that must be reserved for the President’s consideration — preventing the Governor from bypassing the President where assent/review is mandatory.
  • Art 13(2) An ordinance that violates Fundamental Rights is void — the clear textual basis for challenging ordinances.
  • Art 20 Prohibits ex post facto criminal law — so an ordinance cannot create a backdated criminal offence.

How an ordinance ends — and the loophole

Two ways to remove
  • Expiration — hits the constitutional time limit and lapses automatically.
  • Withdrawal — the government actively withdraws it.
The loophole

If the government wants the law to continue but wishes to avoid Parliament’s scrutiny, it may withdraw and immediately re-issue the ordinance — which is exactly the re-promulgation problem.

4WHY — The Rationale for Ordinances

Ordinances exist because certain situations arise where the only viable solution is to create a law, and the regular legislative process is not immediately available.

The structural reason: a session-based legislature

Unlike countries such as Australia, where Parliament effectively functions through the year, the Indian Parliament works in sessions. Between sessions there are periods when no law-making body is active. Ordinances are the mechanism to create law during these gaps when immediate action is needed.

Purpose vs Use — the distinction that elevates an answer

Purpose = the underlying constitutional reason why the power exists (the ‘why’ behind the provision). Use = the practical motive for why it is actually exercised in a given situation. A sophisticated answer separates the two and then asks whether the executive is adhering to the original intent or deviating from it.

Historical criticism

A major criticism is the potential for an extended duration: if an ordinance can run too long, the executive effectively creates and implements law for significant periods without ever seeking Parliament’s approval.

Analytical takeaway (challenging the pandemic narrative)

2019 (16) was actually higher than 2020 (11) or 2021 (15). So the spike in 2021 cannot be explained by COVID alone — the use of ordinances is a long-standing practice, not a pandemic-era phenomenon. The fluctuation itself helps distinguish genuine emergency from habitual bypass.

Historical peaks

  • LPG reforms, 1990–1999: 196 ordinances total; 1993 alone saw 34 — driven by the dual pressure of massive economic shifts and coalition-government complexity. Ordinances were used to bypass the slow parliamentary process for structural reform.
  • Emergency, 1975: ~100 ordinances in a single year — a massive outlier showing how extreme political circumstances scale up executive law-making.
  • ~42 instances where a law began as an ordinance and was later formalised into an Act — the intended “bridge to legislation” function.

State level — some states far higher

Kerala (2006)

Issued 144 ordinances; 141 were successfully converted into Acts — high volume but high conversion (relatively legitimate use).

Bihar

An ordinance was re-issued 256 times — the prime example of re-promulgation misuse to bypass the legislature (the D.C. Wadhwa story).

Variable to correlate: political composition

Don’t stop at counts — correlate frequency with the type of government: minority / majority / supermajority. A government with a strong, stable majority tends to need ordinances less (it can pass laws normally); political instability tends to push frequency up. This lets you argue whether the power is used for genuine necessity or political convenience.

6CURRENT USE — Why They Get Promulgated Today

The original rationale was strictly urgency (filling gaps when Parliament is not sitting). The analytical question is: why has the pattern of usage changed? Moving from Purpose (why they exist in theory) to Use (why they are used in practice) reveals the real drivers.

Political instability
Lack of clear numbers (no majority) makes the normal legislative route harder; ordinances manage immediate needs.
Legislative friction
Strong opposition in the upper house (e.g. Rajya Sabha) creates confrontation the government would rather avoid.
Avoiding scrutiny
Rather than introduce a Bill that triggers debate and possible rejection, the executive promulgates an ordinance — sometimes passing controversial laws within minutes of the House meeting, “without showing their face” to the legislature.
The opposition’s role — a balanced view

Friction is not always the government’s fault. A healthy system needs balance between executive and opposition. Sometimes the opposition prioritises obstructionism — creating hurdles for its own sake. Positions also flip with power: a party may be pro-FDI in government and turn anti-FDI in opposition. This situational shift adds to the friction that pushes the executive toward ordinances.

7The Four Core Issues (the analytical engine)

When evaluating any ordinance dispute, the Supreme Court applies four issues. These are the lens through which the Court examines petitions and the structure you use in answers.

IssueThe questionMapped case law
1. UrgencyWas there a genuine, immediate need requiring action before the legislature could convene?A.K. Roy v. UOI (1982)
2. Re-issue / Re-promulgationWas the ordinance re-issued repeatedly to bypass scrutiny?D.C. Wadhwa v. Bihar (1987) Krishna Kumar Singh (2017)
3. LapseWhat happens to rights/actions created once the ordinance lapses or is withdrawn?Krishna Kumar Singh (2017) + Irreversibility, Public Interest, Constitutional Necessity
4. Judicial Review (JR)How far can the judiciary scrutinise the President’s “satisfaction”?R.C. Cooper v. UOI (1970) S.R. Bommai v. UOI (1994)
Why we even need case law here

The Constitution gives no direct answer to questions like whether an ordinance is a “decorative tool” or whether a specific use is valid. Because the text is not exhaustive on these nuances, Supreme Court judgments supply the legal reasoning that draws the line between legitimate use and misuse.

8Landmark Case Law (chronological evolution)

1955 Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab

The foundational case for understanding the scope and limits of executive power in relation to legislative functions. Start any deep answer on executive law-making here.

1970 R.C. Cooper v. Union of India — Bank Nationalisation case

The bedrock JR case. The Court held that the President’s “satisfaction” is not absolute. It can examine the basis of that satisfaction to check if the decision was malafide (bad faith) or driven by unjust / improper reasons rather than genuine urgency. This prevents the executive from hiding behind “non-reviewable advice” to bypass the legislature through dishonest motives.

1982 A.K. Roy v. Union of India — Issue: Urgency

  • Ordinances must never be a substitute for regular parliamentary legislation.
  • The power is reserved for instances of extreme and unforeseen urgency.
  • Using ordinances for routine lawmaking or to bypass the legislature violates this principle.

1985 / 1987 D.C. Wadhwa v. State of Bihar — Issue: Re-issue

Bihar systematically refused to let ordinances lapse or convert them into Acts — it simply re-issued them. Over 14 years, just 3 ordinances were re-promulgated 256 times, letting the executive bypass the legislature for over a decade.

The verdict

Re-promulgating ordinances without placing them before the legislature is a misuse of power and a violation of the constitutional scheme. The Court called excessive re-promulgation a “fraud on the Constitution” and a subversion of the democratic process.

1994 S.R. Bommai v. Union of India — Issue: JR

Cited alongside R.C. Cooper as the pair anchoring the scope of judicial review over executive “satisfaction.”

2017 Krishna Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar — Re-issue + Lapse

  • The practice of excessive re-promulgation without legislative scrutiny is unconstitutional — a “fraud on the Constitution.”
  • An ordinance cannot be re-promulgated indefinitely; it must be laid before the legislature within six weeks, otherwise it lapses.
  • The executive cannot use ordinance power as a substitute for legislation; ordinances are only for genuine urgency and must be approved by the legislature.
  • Procedural note: the matter went from a three-judge bench and was referred to a five-judge bench for an authoritative ruling; a secondary question was whether the original ordinance was valid at its inception.
  • The judgment is also used for “Lapse” analysis — determining the status of actions taken under a now-lapsed ordinance.
The arc to remember

Ram Jawaya (1955) → R.C. Cooper (1970) → A.K. Roy (1982) → D.C. Wadhwa (1987) → S.R. Bommai (1994) → Krishna Kumar Singh (2017). Also relevant: State of Rajasthan v. Union of India on the extent of JR.

9Judicial Review — Deep Dive

Can the judiciary review an ordinance? Yes — because an ordinance is an executive action that functions as law, it must be open to scrutiny so it does not violate constitutional principles or exceed the power under Art 123 / 213. But the review has a precise boundary.

The real locus of decision-making

Although the President formally exercises the power, the decision is not independent. The President’s “satisfaction” of urgency rests on the advice of the Council of Ministers (CoM) — so the CoM is effectively the entity that decides whether a matter is “urgent” enough.

Council of Ministers  →(advice)→  President’s satisfaction  →(promulgates)→  Ordinance

ADVICE (the input) — NOT reviewable

The content and rationale of the CoM’s advice is shielded by the Separation of Powers. Reviewing it would intrude on the executive’s internal decision-making. This creates a “grey area”: the real drivers (the CoM) are hard to hold accountable through the courts.

SATISFACTION (the outcome) — IS reviewable

The President’s formal “satisfaction” that an ordinance is necessary is subject to JR (R.C. Cooper). The court can check whether it was genuine without second-guessing the political advice that led to it.

Core tension: Judicial Review ↔ Separation of Powers. The distinction between shielded advice and reviewable satisfaction is what lets the courts intervene without crossing into the executive’s domain.

Grounds on which an ordinance can be challenged

  1. Violation of Fundamental Rights — clear basis in Art 13(2).
  2. Doctrine of Colorable Legislation — you cannot do indirectly what you are barred from doing directly. If the legislature lacks the power to pass a law normally, it cannot use an ordinance to achieve the same result.
  3. Lack of Legislative Competence — legislating on a matter where the government has no constitutional authority (e.g. a backdated criminal law).
  4. Malafide / improper motive or lack of genuine necessity — per R.C. Cooper.
The advanced layer: the Irrationality test

The judiciary has moved beyond formal validity to scrutinise the material the President relied on. The CoM must present data/information to justify urgency. If the data referred to does not actually demonstrate a need for urgency, the President’s satisfaction becomes irrational and can be struck down.

Remember: JR is not a critique of the policy itself — it scrutinises the legality and the constitutional process followed during promulgation. The court must always have a valid legal ground to intervene (“on what basis are we reviewing this?”).

Why the difference matters analytically

The Governor’s discretion (often aligned with the Union) makes ordinance power a vivid example that the Centre is stronger than the States — a specific nuance for the Federalism / unitary-bias debate. And re-promulgation at the state level is the prime example of a challenge to Parliamentary Democracy.

11Lapse & Irreversibility

The hardest question: when an ordinance lapses, do the changes it made survive? The answer depends on three decisive factors.

1. Public Interest
Does maintaining the change serve the broader interests of the public?
2. Constitutional Necessity
Is continued application needed to uphold constitutional principles or prevent a legal vacuum?
3. Irreversibility
Have the substantive changes fundamentally altered the legal landscape in a way that cannot easily be undone?
Worked example — criminal law amendment

Suppose an ordinance amends the IPC to criminalise voyeurism, stalking, indecent behaviour and unwelcome advances. Once these acts are criminalised and their legal status has changed, that shift in the legal landscape may persist even after the ordinance lapses, because the underlying legal reality has been fundamentally altered. (Krishna Kumar Singh is the judgment used to analyse this.)

The core concern

A lapse creates legal uncertainty, raising questions of Parliamentary Democracy (is the executive circumventing the legislature?) and Arbitrariness (changes lacking permanent legislative backing).

12Impact on Constitutional Pillars

This is where you convert the technical mechanics into a high-level Mains answer. Misuse of ordinances strikes at multiple pillars at once.

Parliamentary Democracy
The law is presented as a fait accompli — the legislature loses the ability to debate, amend or reject before it takes effect. (Art 20 bars an ordinance from creating retroactive criminal law.)
Separation of Powers
The executive performs the legislature’s function and effectively becomes a lawmaker, blurring the constitutional lines meant to keep the branches distinct and checked.
Federalism
State-level ordinances plus the Governor’s discretion tilt power toward the Centre — a clear instance of India’s unitary bias.
Judicial Review
The necessity and scope of courts’ oversight — checking malafide intent and irrational “satisfaction.”
Higher synthesis — Constitutional Morality

Tie the Core Issues (urgency, lapse, re-issue) and the themes (PD, SoP, Federalism, JR) together to discuss Constitutional Morality — commitment to the spirit and institutional norms of the Constitution, not just the letter. It requires that the executive does not use ordinances to bypass a functional legislature. The doctrine of Colorable Legislation sits inside this: the spirit (legislature as primary lawmaker) must not be undermined by the letter (the emergency provision).

The philosophical clincher (democratic health)

Democracy is not defined only by periodic elections — the true test is how power is exercised in the inter-election period, when the same leadership governs for years. A democratic mandate gives authority, not licence: leaders must rule according to the Constitution and cannot make a “mockery” of it. Arbitrary use of power, even with popular support, erodes institutions over the long term.

14The Mains Answer Framework

To build a high-scoring answer, follow this structured progression. The intro + body build toward the conclusion — the analytical points are not themselves the conclusion.

  1. What are ordinances? — definition + constitutional provisions (Art 123/213). This is your Introduction and the Prelims foundation.
  2. Why do they exist? — purpose / rationale (session gaps, urgency).
  3. Are they common internationally? — only India/Pakistan/Bangladesh (Comparative Constitutionalism / VA angle).
  4. Are they frequent? — support with data (196 in LPG era, 76 in 2010–19, 0 in 2022).
  5. Why are they promulgated today? — current use / drivers (instability, avoiding scrutiny).
  6. What are the core issues? — Urgency, Re-promulgation, Lapse, Role of the Judiciary.
  7. How can they be challenged? — grounds for JR (FR / Art 13(2), Colorable Legislation, Competence).
  8. Impact — Parliamentary Democracy, Separation of Powers, Federalism, Judicial Review, Constitutional Morality.
  9. Cost–Benefit Analysis — weigh immediate utility against long-term constitutional harm.
Benefits
  • Rapid response to emergencies.
  • Legislative continuity when Parliament is not in session.
Harms / Risks
  • Executive overreach.
  • Bypassing legislative scrutiny.
  • Undermining the Rule of Law.
Way forward (avoid a binary yes/no)

Argue that ordinances should be retained — they are a constitutionally recognised mechanism for genuine exigency — but must be used with extreme caution and strict adherence to safeguards. Then elevate the discussion from a legalistic argument to a philosophical one: how the use (or misuse) of such powers affects the health and integrity of democracy. Add an ethics / merit lens: is the practice ethically justifiable even where technically legal?

Critical closing question: Do the benefits of issuing an ordinance outweigh the harm caused to the constitutional structure?
